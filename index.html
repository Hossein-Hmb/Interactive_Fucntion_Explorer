Uncertainty Principle

The uncertainty principle is an important concept in quantum mechanics. This principle states that one cannot know the exact measurement of the position $x$ and momentum $p$ of a particle. You can only know one or the other. If for example you know the momentum and you try to find the position using wave equation, you get a probability of where that particle might have been at time $t$.

\[ \Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \]

Wigner Function

The Wigner function is a quasi-probability distribution function used in quantum mechanics to represent the quantum state of a system. It provides a way to visualize the phase space of a quantum system, allowing for the analysis of its properties and dynamics. The Wigner function is defined in terms of the position $x$ and momentum $p$ variables, and it can take on negative values, which is a characteristic feature of quantum mechanics.

\[ W_n(x,p) = \frac{(-1)^n}{\pi}\,e^{-(x^2 + p^2)}\,L_n\bigl(2(x^2 + p^2)\bigr) \]

Gaussian Wigner Function 2D

The Gaussian Wigner function is a specific form of the Wigner function that describes a Gaussian state in phase space. It is characterized by its mean position $x$ and momentum $p$. The Gaussian Wigner function is defined :

\[ W(r) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{\det(\gamma)}}\,e^{-\tfrac12\,r^T\gamma^{-1}r} \]

where $r$ is a column vector of the position and momentum variables of two particles $A$ and $B$, and $\gamma$ is the covariance matrix. Note that $\gamma$ must satisfy the uncertainty principle, which states that the determinant of the covariance matrix must be greater than or equal to $\frac{\hbar^2}{4}$.

Gaussian Wigner Function 4D

The Gaussian Wigner function in four dimensions extends the concept of the 2D Gaussian Wigner function to higher dimensions. It is used to represent more complex quantum states and their interactions. The 4D Gaussian Wigner function is defined similarly to the 2D case, but it incorporates two states $A$ and $B$, each represented by a $2\times2$ covariance matrix. As such, $\gamma$ is now a $4\times4$ matrix and the vector $r$ is 4 dimensional. The Wigner function is given by:

\[ W(r) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{\det(\gamma)}}\,e^{-\tfrac12\,r^T\gamma^{-1}r} \]

Coupling Matrix

The coupling matrix describes the interaction between different quantum states or modes. It is essential for understanding the dynamics of quantum systems and their evolution over time.

Let us assume we have two states $A$ and $B$ and they respect the uncertainty principle given by:

\[A^\pm = A \pm \frac{i\Omega}{2}, \Omega = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1\\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

The coupling matrix is a $4\times4$ matrix that describes the interaction between the two states. It is defined as:

\[ \gamma\Pi = \begin{bmatrix} A & X^T\\ X & B \end{bmatrix} \]

where $X$ is the covariance matrix of the two states given by:

\[|X| \le \sqrt{\sqrt{A^\pm}\times B\times\sqrt{A^\pm}}\]

With the coupling matrix, we can analyse the interactions between the two states and how they influence each other. It shows the degree to which changes in one state affects the other state.

Wasserstein Distance

The Wasserstein distance is a metric used to measure the distance between two probability distributions. In the context of quantum mechanics, it can be used to quantify the differences between quantum states represented by their Wigner functions.

\[ W(\rho_A, \rho_B)^2 = \frac{1}{2}TrA+\frac{1}{2}TrB-Tr[X] \]

where $A$ and $B$ are the covariance matrices of the two states, and $X$ is given by:

\[|X| \le \sqrt{\sqrt{A^\pm}\times B\times\sqrt{A^\pm}}\]

The Wasserstein distance is a powerful tool for comparing quantum states and analyzing their properties. It is particularly useful in quantum information theory and quantum optics. For example, the Wasserstein distance can demonstrate the optimal path between two states $A$ and $B$ where the value of the cost function is at a minimum.
